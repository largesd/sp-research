### 1. Executive Summary  

Hong Kong faces a unique confluence of vulnerabilities in the post-AI era: a service-dominated economy with high exposure to AI automation (Paul; Mary), an aging population with elevated reskilling costs (Mary), limited social safety nets (Ming), and deep integration with mainland China’s rapidly digitizing economy. Research indicates that 40–60% of jobs in advanced economies like Hong Kong are exposed to AI automation, with administrative, legal, finance, and engineering roles at highest risk (Mary; Paul). While AI may create new roles in care, education, and AI-augmented services, the transition period (2028–2032) could trigger structural unemployment, wage polarization, and social instability without proactive intervention.  

This proposal synthesizes evidence from three research streams to recommend a three-horizon strategy:  
- **Short-term (1–2 years):** Stabilize labor markets through wage insurance, skills accounts, and real-time monitoring.  
- **Mid-term (3–5 years):** Scale lifelong learning, portable benefits, and human-AI collaboration incentives.  
- **Long-term (5+ years):** Institutionalize just transition governance, AI taxation, and income de-linking mechanisms.  

All measures are tailored to Hong Kong’s context—leveraging its education emphasis, fiscal capacity, and proximity to mainland innovation ecosystems—while avoiding politically unfeasible universal basic income (UBI) in favor of targeted, evidence-based interventions.

### 3. Short-term Policies (1–2 years)  

#### Policy Measures  
1. **Launch a Hong Kong Transition Monitoring Unit (HK-TMU)**  
   - Real-time tracking of AI adoption, job displacement risks, and skill gaps using labor market data and employer surveys (Paul).  
2. **Introduce Wage Insurance for Mid-Career Workers**  
   - Subsidize 50% of income loss for workers over 45 transitioning to lower-paying roles due to AI, capped at HK$10,000/month for 12 months (Paul; Ming).  
3. **Pilot Individual Learning Accounts (ILAs)**  
   - Provide HK$5,000/year in portable credits to all adults for approved training, modeled on Singapore’s SkillsFuture (Paul; Ming).  
4. **Mandate AI Impact Disclosure for Large Employers**  
   - Require firms with >200 employees to report AI deployment plans and reskilling commitments (Paul; Ming).  

#### Expected Outcomes  
- Early warning of sectoral shocks (e.g., finance automation).  
- Reduced income volatility for displaced professionals.  
- Increased training participation among at-risk workers.  

#### Resource Requirements  
- HK$500 million/year for wage insurance (covering ~5,000 workers).  
- HK$300 million/year for ILAs (assuming 600,000 active adults).  
- HK$50 million for HK-TMU staffing and data infrastructure.

### 5. Long-term Policies (5+ years)  

#### Policy Measures  
1. **Enact a Hong Kong AI Governance Act**  
   - Regulate algorithmic management (e.g., hiring, performance evaluation) with human oversight and appeal rights, aligned with EU AI Act principles (Paul; Ming).  
2. **Implement a Data Dividend and AI Rent Tax**  
   - Levy a 5% tax on corporate profits derived from AI systems using public or personal data, funding lifelong learning and social transfers (Mary; Ming).  
3. **Adopt a “Just Transition” Framework**  
   - Legislate worker representation on corporate AI ethics boards and establish district-level transition councils for affected communities (Paul; Ming).  
4. **Pilot a Participation Income Scheme**  
   - Replace unemployment benefits with conditional payments for community service, caregiving, or training, avoiding UBI’s fiscal burden (Mary).  

#### Expected Outcomes  
- Ethical AI deployment aligned with social values.  
- Sustainable funding for human capital investment.  
- Inclusive governance preventing elite capture of AI benefits.

### 6. Implementation Considerations  

#### Political Feasibility  
- **Strengths**: Hong Kong’s technocratic governance, strong rule of law, and business-friendly environment support regulatory innovation (e.g., AI Act).  
- **Challenges**: Resistance from finance sector to AI disclosure and taxation; public skepticism toward welfare expansion given limited tradition.  
- **Strategy**: Frame policies as “human capital investment” rather than welfare; leverage Chief Executive’s 2023 policy address on AI development.  

#### Budget Implications  
- Short-term costs (HK$850 million/year) are manageable given Hong Kong’s HK$900 billion fiscal reserves.  
- Mid/long-term measures are largely self-financing via AI rent tax and payroll levies.  
- Avoid UBI due to Mary’s finding that it would require >15% of GDP (HK$450 billion/year)—fiscally unsustainable.  

#### Stakeholder Impacts  
- **Workers**: Enhanced security and mobility, especially for aging professionals.  
- **Employers**: Short-term compliance costs offset by tax incentives and skilled labor supply.  
- **Government**: Requires cross-bureau coordination (Labour, Education, Innovation).  

#### Risk Mitigation  
- **Capital Flight**: Design AI tax narrowly on data-derived profits, not general automation.  
- **Training Mismatch**: HK-TMU ensures ILAs align with real-time skill demands.  
- **Mainland Competition**: Partner with Guangdong on cross-border reskilling programs to retain talent.
